\section{Foundation Models \& Structure-Preserving AI for Kinetic Equations}

\noindent\textit{Why it matters.}\enspace
Kinetic equations are among the most computationally expensive models in all
of science.  A single RTE simulation in nuclear reactor transport involves
$10^8$--$10^{12}$ phase-space unknowns, with run times on classical
supercomputers measured in hours.  Fusion plasma simulations face a starker
challenge: real-time feedback control of a tokamak plasma requires millions
of Vlasov--Maxwell evaluations per second, a throughput that is five to six
orders of magnitude beyond what current particle-in-cell (PIC) codes can
deliver.  Equally important is the \emph{multiscale} structure of these
equations: in the limit $\varepsilon \to 0$ (small Knudsen number), the
kinetic model degenerates to a macroscopic diffusion or fluid system, but
classical numerical discretizations become stiff and impose a time-step
restriction $\Delta t = O(\varepsilon)$, adding an entire order of
computational difficulty to an already intractable problem.

Neural operators have emerged as a powerful paradigm for learning PDEs, offering capabilities that extend beyond traditional numerical solvers to modeling physical phenomena directly from experimental data. Concurrently, integrating physical constraints into machine learning models---popularized by architectures like PINNs and the DeepRitz method---has proven essential to significantly reduce the dependency on massive datasets. These twin principles drive our research program: first, developing \textbf{DeepRTE} to directly capture the complex structural mappings inherent in neural operators; and second, designing \textbf{Asymptotic-Preserving Neural Networks (APNNs)} to rigorously embed physical constraints into the training process, guaranteeing physical fidelity in multiscale regimes.

\subsection{DeepRTE: A Foundation Model for Radiative Transfer}

\emph{My most recent and important work} is the development of
\textbf{DeepRTE}~\cite{DeepRTE2026}, an efficient neural operator approach
for the steady-state Radiative Transfer Equation (RTE). The RTE is a diﬀerential-integral
equation that governs the propagation of radiation through a participating
medium, with applications spanning diverse domains such as neutron transport,
atmospheric radiative transfer, heat transfer, and optical imaging.
Classical deterministic methods typically involve discretizing the equation
in both spatial and angular variables, which is computationally expensive and
is considered as one of the core tasks in high-performance computing.
Our DeepRTE framework demonstrates superior computational eﬃciency for solving
the steady-state RTE, surpassing traditional methods and existing neural
network approaches. This eﬃciency is achieved by embedding physical
information through derivation of the RTE and mathematically-informed network
architecture, and using a mesh-free neural operator framework with inherent
zero-shot capability.

This represents a qualitative shift in computational physics: rather than an
isolated scheme for an individual problem, DeepRTE provides the community
with a \emph{reusable computational asset} that amortizes the immense initial
training cost across thousands of downstream, domain-specific tasks.

\subsection{Asymptotic-Preserving Neural Networks Framework}

The central contribution of my group in this area is the development of the \emph{Asymptotic-Preserving Neural Networks} (APNNs) framework. This is not merely a single model, but a fundamentally new series of physics-informed methodologies to address multiscale problems and asymptotic-preserving (AP) properties.

The key insight driving this framework is that when using neural networks to solve PDEs, the equations are inherently transformed into optimization problems. Consequently, the hard constraints of classical solutions and parameters are relaxed into soft constraints. In a data-driven setting or within pure PINN setups, such soft constraints fail drastically in extreme multiscale regimes (e.g., Knudsen number $\varepsilon \to 0$), leading to severe performance degradation due to spectral bias and stiffness.

To rectify this, APNNs decouple the multiscale constraints. We incorporate the micro--macro decomposition algebraically into the overall construction, forming multiple or even redundant equations to guide the relaxed optimization safely toward the correct limit. Essential physical properties---such as mass/density conservation or Maxwellian convergence---are inherently satisfied as \textbf{hard architectural constraints}. This formulation guarantees that even with highly limited labeled data and the presence of optimization inaccuracies, the learned network will naturally preserve uniform accuracy across all scales and correctly degenerate to the macroscopic limits. The framework is flexible and not bound merely to PINNs; it thrives seamlessly with DeepRitz or general neural-operator paradigms.

The framework has successfully revolutionized the treatment of numerous canonical multiscale kinetic systems. We summarize these works below:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Basic APNN for Linear Transport}~\cite{APNN2023,APNN2024}: We first established the APNN framework using the even-odd decomposition for linear transport equations. We proved uniform accuracy from the kinetic regime to the diffusion limit. The network achieves optimal performance on problems where standard PINNs fail completely for $\varepsilon < 10^{-3}$, without relying on strict mesh refinement.

  \item \textbf{APNN for Non-linear Systems (VPFP)}~\cite{APNN-VPFP2024}: Moving to high-field regimes, we extended APNNs to the Vlasov--Poisson--Fokker--Planck system. By adapting the loss formulation and microscopic structures, the updated network captures AP behavior under complex, long-range electromagnetic self-interactions.

  \item \textbf{Operator-Learning variants (AP-CON, GRTE-APNN)}~\cite{APCON2024, Wu2025}: Integrating operator theories, we constructed variants like AP-CON for Gray radiative transfer using convolutional DeepONets. These advanced pipelines generalize across $10^4$ distinct optical configurations seamlessly, reaching classical $S_N$ solver accuracy but operating $10^3\times$ faster in inference.

  \item \textbf{Recurrent Temporal Extensions (RT-APNN)}~\cite{RTAPNN2025}: For pure time-dependent analysis, we developed Recurrent Temporal APNNs, employing GRU mechanisms with physical priors to capture long-time dynamics smoothly without suffering instability across varied timescales.

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Asymptotic-Preserving Random Feature Methods}

Beyond neural networks, in collaboration with Jingrun~Chen (USTC), we
developed an \emph{asymptotic-preserving random feature method}
(AP-RFM)~\cite{AP-RFM2025} for multiscale radiative transfer.  Random
feature methods expand the solution in a random basis
$\sigma(\omega_j \cdot x + b_j)$ with fixed random weights, offering
mesh-free approximation with theoretically controllable error at
$O(N)$ complexity.  By encoding the micro--macro decomposition into both
the basis construction and the least-squares fitting, AP-RFM achieves uniform
accuracy across all Knudsen numbers at a cost substantially lower than neural
network training.  This result demonstrates that the AP design principle is
a general mathematical framework---not a property specific to any particular
machine learning architecture.
